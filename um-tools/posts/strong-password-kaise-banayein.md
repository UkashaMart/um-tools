# How to Create a Strong Password

Online security is more important than ever. A weak password is the number one reason accounts get hacked. In this guide, we cover everything you need to know about creating and managing strong passwords.

## What Makes a Password Strong?

A strong password has these key features:

- **Length:** At least 12–16 characters — longer is always better
- **Mix of characters:** Uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special symbols
- **Unique:** Never reuse the same password across multiple accounts
- **Random:** Avoid using your name, birthday, or any personal information

## Common Password Mistakes to Avoid

Many people use passwords that are extremely easy to guess. Here are the most common mistakes:

❌ `password123` — One of the most used passwords in the world  
❌ `Ali1990` — Contains personal information  
❌ `123456` — Completely predictable  
❌ `iloveyou` — Found in every hacker wordlist  
❌ `qwerty` — Keyboard pattern, very easy to guess  

## Examples of Strong Passwords

✅ `K8#mP2$vL9@nQ5`  
✅ `Xy7!rB3&wN1*tF`  
✅ `9@Lp!mQ3#zR7$kW`  

Notice how these are completely random with no recognizable words or patterns.

## How to Remember Strong Passwords

The best way to manage strong passwords is to use a **password manager** such as Bitwarden (free) or 1Password. These tools store all your passwords securely so you only need to remember one master password.

Another method is the **passphrase technique** — combine 4 random words with symbols:  
`Horse!Cloud9River#Moon`  
This is long, easy to remember, and very hard to crack.

## Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Even the strongest password can be stolen through phishing. Always enable **two-factor authentication (2FA)** on your important accounts — Gmail, social media, and banking apps. This adds a second layer of security even if your password is compromised.

## How Often Should You Change Your Password?

Security experts now recommend changing passwords only when:
- You suspect your account has been compromised
- A service you use reports a data breach
- You have been sharing a password with someone

Changing passwords too frequently often leads to weaker passwords because people start using predictable patterns.
